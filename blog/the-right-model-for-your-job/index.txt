1b:T538b,<p class="blog-md-p">The Right Tool for the Job: A Guide to AI Models by Data Type</p>
<p class="blog-md-p">In the world of AI runtimes like Foundry, LM Studio, and OpenAI, we often talk about "models" as a single category. But just as you wouldn't use a calculator to write a novel, different AI models are built to handle specific types of data.</p>
<p class="blog-md-p">If you are building an application or setting up a local workflow, understanding these categories is the first step to success. Here is a breakdown of AI models categorized by the data they process.</p>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h2 id="a-text-x26-language-models-the-brains" class="blog-md-h2">A) Text &#x26; Language Models (The "Brains")</h2>
<p class="blog-md-p">These are the most common models you’ll encounter in LM Studio and OpenAI. They process strings of text to understand intent or create content.</p>
<h3 id="1-chatllms-large-language-models" class="blog-md-h3">1) Chat/LLMs (Large Language Models)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">Designed for conversation and reasoning.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Examples</strong>: Llama 3 (Meta), phi-4 (Microsoft), GPT-4o (OpenAI).</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Writing emails, coding, or summarizing a long report.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="2-embedding-models" class="blog-md-h3">2) Embedding Models</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">These turn text into math (vectors) so computers can "search" by meaning rather than just keywords.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Examples</strong>: nomic-embed-text, text-embedding-3-small.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Powering a search engine that understands what you mean, even if you don't use the exact words.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h2 id="b-vision-models-the-eyes" class="blog-md-h2">B) Vision Models (The "Eyes")</h2>
<p class="blog-md-p">Vision models allow AI to interpret the physical world through pixels. These are becoming standard in runtimes like Foundry.</p>
<h3 id="1-vision-language-models-vlm" class="blog-md-h3">1) Vision-Language Models (VLM)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">These are "multimodal," meaning they can look at an image and then talk about it in a chat interface.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Examples</strong>: Qwen2.5-VL, GPT-4o with Vision.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Uploading a screenshot of a complex chart and asking the AI to "Explain the trend in this data."</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="2-image-generation" class="blog-md-h3">2) Image Generation</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">These turn text descriptions into brand-new visuals.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Examples</strong>: DALL·E 3, Stable Diffusion.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h2 id="c-audio-x26-speech-models-the-ears-x26-voice" class="blog-md-h2">C) Audio &#x26; Speech Models (The "Ears &#x26; Voice")</h2>
<p class="blog-md-p">These models bridge the gap between sound waves and digital text.</p>
<h3 id="1-speech-to-text-stt" class="blog-md-h3">1) Speech-to-Text (STT)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">High-speed transcription.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Example</strong>: Whisper (OpenAI).</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Automatically creating subtitles for a video or transcribing a meeting in real-time.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="2-text-to-speech-tts" class="blog-md-h3">2) Text-to-Speech (TTS)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">Turning digital text into natural human voices.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Narrating an audiobook or providing a voice for a virtual assistant.</li>
</ul>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h2 id="d-reasoning-x26-structured-data-models-the-logic" class="blog-md-h2">D) Reasoning &#x26; Structured Data Models (The "Logic")</h2>
<p class="blog-md-p">Sometimes you don't need a chatty AI; you need a logical one that can handle math or massive tables of data.</p>
<h3 id="1-reasoning-models" class="blog-md-h3">1) Reasoning Models</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">These use "Chain of Thought" processing. They pause to "think" through a logical sequence before giving an answer.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Examples</strong>: DeepSeek-R1, o1-preview.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Use Case</strong>: Solving complex math problems or debugging deep architectural flaws in code.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="2-rerank-models" class="blog-md-h3">2) Rerank Models</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">These specialize in sorting. They take a list of data and re-order it based on exact relevance.</p>
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Example</strong>: Cohere-rerank.</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="g-example-designing-a-private-corporate-brain" class="blog-md-h2">G) Example: Designing a Private Corporate Brain</h2>
<p class="blog-md-p">Let’s design a Private Corporate Brain—a system that allows employees to upload internal documents (PDFs, Excel, Wiki pages) and ask questions about them securely.
This is a classic RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) system. Here is the high-level design broken down by the flow of data.</p>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h3 id="phase-1-the-ingestion-pipeline-data-to-math" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 1: The Ingestion Pipeline (Data to Math)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">This happens behind the scenes before you ever start chatting.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: You upload a 100-page HR Policy PDF.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>Parser</strong>: A script (like PyPDF) reads the text.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Chunker</strong>: The system breaks the 100 pages into 500 small "snippets" of 200 words each.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Model Used</strong>: Embedding Model.
<ul>
<li><strong>Choice</strong>: nomic-embed-text (Local/Foundry) or text-embedding-3 (OpenAI).</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Role</strong>: It turns each snippet into a vector (a string of numbers).</li>
</ul>
</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Storage</strong>: These vectors are saved in a Vector Database (like ChromaDB or Pinecone).</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h3 id="phase-2-the-retrieval-step-search" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 2: The Retrieval Step (Search)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">This happens the moment you type a question.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: You type: "What is the maternity leave policy?"</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>Model Used</strong>: Embedding Model (Same one as Phase 1).
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: It turns your question into a vector.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Vector Search</strong>: The system compares your "question vector" against the "snippet vectors" in the database.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Result</strong>: It pulls the top 3 most mathematically similar snippets from your HR PDF.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h3 id="phase-3-the-generation-step-the-answer" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 3: The Generation Step (The Answer)</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">This is where the AI actually "talks."</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: The UI displays a natural language answer.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>The Prompt</strong>: The system sends a hidden instruction to the LLM: "Here is some context from our files: [Snippet 1, Snippet 2]. Based ONLY on this, answer the user's question: What is the maternity leave policy?"</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Model Used</strong>: Chat/LLM Model.
<ul>
<li><strong>Choice</strong>: phi-4 (Local/Foundry) for privacy, or gpt-4o (OpenAI) for complex logic.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Role</strong>: It reads the context and writes a polite, accurate response.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p class="blog-md-p">This product is an Automated Data Extraction Pipeline (often called an "Intelligent Document Processor" or IDP). It is designed to take "messy" data like an email thread or a photo of a crumpled receipt and turn it into a clean, machine-readable JSON file that your accounting software can understand.</p>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h3 id="phase-1-the-input-x26-recognition-the-eyes" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 1: The Input &#x26; Recognition (The "Eyes")</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">Before the data can be turned into JSON, the system must "see" it properly.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: An employee uploads a photo of a taxi receipt or forwards a vendor email.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>OCR (Optical Character Recognition)</strong>: If it’s an image, the system extracts the text.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Vision Model (VLM)</strong>: Modern systems use a vision model to understand the layout (e.g., recognizing that the number at the bottom right is the "Total").
<ul>
<li><strong>Model Used</strong>: Vision-Language Model.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Choice</strong>: gpt-4o (OpenAI) or Qwen2-VL (Local/LM Studio).
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: It looks at the image and converts it into a structured text description.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>
<hr class="blog-md-hr" />
<h3 id="phase-2-schema-based-parsing-the-logic" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 2: Schema-Based Parsing (The "Logic")</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">This is where the unstructured text is forced into a specific JSON format (e.g., {"vendor": "Uber", "amount": 25.50}).</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: The system maps the text to your database fields.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>Function Calling / Structured Output</strong>: You provide the model with a JSON Schema (a blueprint of the fields you need).
<ul>
<li><strong>Model Used</strong>: Reasoning or Small Language Model (SLM).</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Choice</strong>: gpt-4o-mini (OpenAI) for high accuracy/low cost, or phi-4 (Foundry) for local processing.
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: It acts as a "translator," ignoring the fluff (like "Regards, John") and extracting only the required keys: Date, Vendor, Total, and Currency.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p class="blog-md-p">AI can sometimes hallucinate, so the system must validate the JSON against your business rules.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: The system flags a receipt if the "Tax" + "Subtotal" does not equal the "Total."</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>Code Interpreter / Logic Layer</strong>: A simple Python script validates the math.
<ul>
<li><strong>Model Used</strong>: Reasoning Model.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Choice</strong>: o1-mini (OpenAI) or DeepSeek-R1 (Local).
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: If the validation fails, this model "thinks" through the error to see if it misread a number (e.g., mistaking an '8' for a 'B').</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<p class="blog-md-p">The system first needs to turn raw audio waves into highly accurate text, filtering out background noise and "umms/ahhs."</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: You tap a button and say: "Hey, remind me to follow up with Sarah from Hooli about the API migration, set a high priority, and link it to the Q3 Project."</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>STT (Speech-to-Text)</strong>: The audio file is sent to a transcription engine.
<ul>
<li><strong>Diarization</strong>: If multiple people are talking, the system identifies "Who said what."</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Model Used</strong>: Audio/Speech Model.
<ul>
<li><strong>Choice</strong>: Whisper (OpenAI) or Deepgram.
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: It creates a perfect text transcript of your voice.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="phase-2-intent-extraction-the-brain" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 2: Intent Extraction (The "Brain")</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">This is the most critical step. The AI must figure out that "Sarah from Hooli" is a Contact, "API migration" is the Subject, and "High" is the Priority.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: The system maps your "messy" speech into a structured format.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>NER (Named Entity Recognition)</strong>: The model identifies people, companies, and dates.
<ul>
<li><strong>Instruction Following</strong>: The model is told: "Extract the task, the person involved, and the urgency from this text."</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>Model Used</strong>: Chat/LLM Model (Optimized for Extraction).
<ul>
<li><strong>Choice</strong>: gpt-4o-mini (OpenAI), Claude 3.5 Haiku (Anthropic), or phi-4 (Foundry).
<ul>
<li><strong>Role</strong>: It outputs a JSON block like {"task": "Follow up", "contact": "Sarah", "urgency": "High"}.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ol>

<h3 id="phase-3-the-execution-the-hands" class="blog-md-h3">Phase 3: The Execution (The "Hands")</h3>
<p class="blog-md-p">The structured data is then "pushed" into the actual software tools you use.</p>
<ol class="blog-md-ol">
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>The Product Action</strong>: A new ticket appears in your project management tool (like Jira, Trello, or Notion) with all fields pre-filled.</li>
<li class="blog-md-li"><strong>System Design</strong>:
<ul class="blog-md-ul">
<li><strong>Function Calling / Tool Use</strong>: The AI model is given access to "Tools" (APIs). It doesn't just write the text; it triggers a command like create_jira_ticket(title, priority).
<ul>
<li><strong>Model Used</strong>: Agentic/Action Model.
<ul>
